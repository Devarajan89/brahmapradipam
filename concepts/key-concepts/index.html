Key Concepts of Advaita Vedanta

Core teachings of non-dual philosophy

Advaita Vedanta is the non-dualistic school of Vedanta philosophy, primarily established by Adi Shankaracharya. It teaches that Brahman (ultimate reality) and Atman (individual self) are identical, and that the perception of duality is due to ignorance (avidya).

1. The Nature of Reality (Brahman)

Brahman is the sole reality. It is described as:

  • ‚Ä¢ Ananda (‡§Ü‡§®‡§®‡•ç‡§¶) - Pure Bliss, limitless and complete

Brahman is nirguna (without attributes) and nirvisesha (without distinctions) in its absolute nature, yet appears with attributes (saguna) due to maya.

2. The Individual Self (Atman)

Atman is your true nature, identical with Brahman:

  • ‚Ä¢ Not the body, which is gross and perishable
  • ‚Ä¢ Not the mind, which is subtle but still changing
  • ‚Ä¢ Not the intellect, which is an instrument of knowledge
  • ‚Ä¢ Pure consciousness, the witness of all experiences

"That thou art" (Tat Tvam Asi) - The great Upanishadic teaching affirming the identity of Atman and Brahman.

3. Maya and the Appearance of the World

Maya is the mysterious power by which the One appears as many:

  • ‚Ä¢ Not absolutely real (sat) like Brahman
  • ‚Ä¢ Not absolutely unreal (asat) like a fantasy
  • ‚Ä¢ Inexplicable (anirvachaniya) - neither existent nor non-existent
  • ‚Ä¢ Has two powers: avarana shakti (veiling) and vikshepa shakti (projecting)

The world is mithya - apparently real but ultimately dependent on Brahman for its existence, like a rope appearing as a snake in dim light.

4. Ignorance (Avidya) and Liberation (Moksha)

Avidya (ignorance) is the root cause of all suffering:

  • ‚Ä¢ Causes us to identify with the body-mind complex
  • ‚Ä¢ Creates the sense of separation and duality
  • ‚Ä¢ Leads to endless desires and karmic bondage

Moksha (liberation) comes through:

  • ‚Ä¢ Shravana - Listening to the teachings from a qualified guru
  • ‚Ä¢ Manana - Reflecting on the teachings to remove doubts
  • ‚Ä¢ Nididhyasana - Deep meditation on the truth

Liberation is not achieving something new, but recognizing what you already are - pure consciousness, always free, always whole.

5. The Three States of Consciousness

Every individual experiences three states:

  • Waking (Jagrat) - Experience of the gross world through senses and mind
  • Dream (Swapna) - Experience of a subtle world created by the mind
  • Deep Sleep (Sushupti) - State of no experience, where individuality temporarily dissolves

Turiya (the fourth) is not a state but the unchanging consciousness that witnesses all three states - this is your true nature.

6. The Path of Knowledge (Jnana Yoga)

Qualifications (Sadhana Chatushtaya):

  • ‚Ä¢ Viveka - Discrimination between eternal and non-eternal
  • ‚Ä¢ Vairagya - Dispassion towards worldly pleasures
  • ‚Ä¢ Shatsampat - Six virtues (control of mind, control of senses, withdrawal, forbearance, faith, concentration)
  • ‚Ä¢ Mumukshutva - Intense desire for liberation

Only a student with these qualifications is fit to receive the direct teaching of Brahman from a qualified guru.
